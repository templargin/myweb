Work

March 2017

 

Last week on March 9th it had been two years since I joined the corporate office world. Two years of being a professional, white-collar, office rat, a cog in a machine, or however else you want to call it.

 

It is a good time for me to stop for a while and take a look back at these two years, take a deep breath and evaluate what was gained, what was lost and what was learned.

 

When it comes to evaluating, in my opinion, it is wise to divide your life into categories. This way helps the evaluation process because it is very difficult to be objective unless you do so. People tend to have different categories, but they more or less boil down to variations of these few: professional, financial, personal, social, and emotional. Maybe I will add some later on. These are what come to mind at the moment and I’ll cover a few.

 

Professional

The major learning curve goes around the first 6-8 months. After that, you have to force yourself to learn. If you don't want to you can easily get away by just repeatedly doing what you already now. It is quite scary to see people around that were doing the same exact thing for 15-20 years.

 

It is quite amazing that the "professional" world is much less meticulous and much sloppier than academia. When you go to school nobody tells you about how the real world works: adults are just figuring most of the things out on the fly. 

 

There is a lot of complaining and finger-pointing. Nobody wants to take responsibility, nobody wants to take action, but if you talk to each person individually everyone seems to know what exactly is wrong with the company and how to improve it. There is definitely a "Persian messenger" syndrome around - nobody wants to deliver bad news and keep those who are higher up happy by false reporting the evidence.


The salary is not going to go up unless you ask for it. And in order to be able to ask you have to be confident you deserve a raise. I will stop here. There can be a separate document on office life observations only. Maybe next time.


All that being said, I was lucky with my department. The people I directly work and interact with are great. To summarize: I like most of the people, I dislike the environment.


Financial

I am young, not married, no kids. This allows me to live a very decent life with occasional frivolous expenditures and still being able to save some money. Salary is not going to make you rich even if it is a high salary (which I don't have). You exchange some of your time for money (laundry, car wash, car fixes, dinner out, etc.). One has to create a separate account to put away money. Otherwise, no matter how strong you think your willpower is, you will spend more than you need. Better not to see that money at all.

 

Emotional

Here comes the hardest part - happiness. This thing gets destroyed pretty quickly with work, tiredness, uselessness, and just a general feeling of being “not as happy”. You have to actually work hard for happiness. It doesn’t take any effort to be a miserable loser.
